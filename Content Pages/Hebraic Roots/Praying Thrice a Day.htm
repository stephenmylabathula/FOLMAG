Praying Thrice a Day... But WHY?

Oct-Dec.2013

 

Tikkun olam is a Hebrew phrase which means ‘repairing or healing the world.’ Tikkun

olam is included in the Aleinu, which is a Jewish prayer that is traditionally recited

three times daily by practicing Jews. Aleinu is said to have been written by Joshua after

conquering Jericho. Aleinu in Hebrew means ‘it is our duty,’ or Aleinu leshabei’ach, [it is]

our duty to praise.

 

Jews are expected to be Jews 24/7, which means their Jewishness must be lived more

outside than in the precincts of a religious center or within the four walls of one’s home.

Gratitude and thanking to God are inseparable to Jews. They pray for almost everything,

right from the time they wake up in the morning: in the bathroom, coming out of the

bathroom, for putting on tzitzit [specially-knotted ritual fringes], for putting on a talit

katan [an everyday undergarment], for putting on a tallit gadol [a prayer shawl], on

inspection of the tzitzit, before putting on the tallit, after wrapping the tallit around the

body, for putting on tefillin [a set of small black leather boxes containing scrolls of

parchment inscribed with verses from the Torah], on placement of the arm-tefillin, on

placement of the head-tefillin, on coiling the straps around the middle finger, blessings

during a meal, before washing of hands, blessing [birkot] over the bread before and after

the meal, blessings over food, before eating grain products, before drinking wine, before

eating fruit, before eating non-fruit produce, and before eating other foods.” To keep up

with a constant communion with God, Jews pray three times a day. David and Daniel

prayed thrice a day [Psalms 55:17 & Daniel 6:10]. The three prayers are Shacharit, Mincha,

and Maariv.

 

The Shacharit prayer takes place in the morning. The Hebrew word Shacharit comes

from the root word, Shachar, which means dawn or morning. The timing is usually when

there is enough sun or natural light to recognise an acquaintance at six feet distance.

However, Shacharit prayers are also offered between the sunrise and midday, too. The

prayers contain within it the basic affirmations of Judaism, the Shema, the Amidah and

the ideas of repentance, self-introspection and loyalty to God and Israel.

Shacharit is said to be started by Abraham in Genesis 22:3, which is traditionally the

first Shacharit. The sages attribute the origin of these three daily prayer services to the

patriarchs; Abraham created the prayer time for Shacharit, Isaac for Mincha, and Jacob

for Maariv.

 

Mincha is connected with being ‘in the field.’ The Shacharit and evening Maariv prayers

may be prayed outside of the time constraints of the mundane everyday tasks. Not so

with Mincha. It stops the Jews in the middle of their work, shopping, school, and all

other usual tasks that life places upon them. But it is an oasis of spiritual time in a tough

workday, a moment of contemplation, a calming of nerves and focusing on priorities. As

such, it is perhaps the most important and meaningful prayer service of the day.

Maariv is the prayer of the night. Also called arvit prayer, it takes place at night after

sunset. According to Lazer Gurkow, “When evening falls, we sense the mortality of the

human condition; we realise that daylight and warmth don’t last forever. The morning’s

optimism and the afternoon’s momentum give way to the night’s brooding despair. As

the day wanes, we wrap up our activities and wearily sit back to reflect. Though the day

has been successful, we realise that there is yet so much to achieve. We wanted to

complete the task but darkness has encroached.”

 

Why would Jacob have instituted this evening prayer? Gurkow continues, “As the sun

set on his first day of travel, the Torah tells, ‘he encountered the place’ where the Temple

was later built. What did he do at that space? He composed the evening service.”
